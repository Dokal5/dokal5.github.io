Thoughts Essay

Feedback Verbs Are Not Interchangeable

One thing I notice often in supervision and coaching is this: people receive feedback, but they do not know how to respond to it. The problem is usually not effort. The problem is translation.

Core idea
If you do not understand the feedback verb, you do not understand the expected kind of improvement.
They may sound similar, but each feedback verb does a different job in building a strong argument.
Framework

The five response jobs

People often read comments like “explain more” and “justify this point” as if they are equivalent. They are not. Each verb asks for a distinct upgrade in the quality of thinking and communication.

01

Explain

Define what you mean, so the claim is clear and unambiguous.

02

Elaborate

Add relevant context, so the claim is properly situated.

03

Show Reasoning

Make the causal logic explicit from premise to conclusion.

04

Justify

Defend why this is the right choice among alternatives.

05

Substantiate

Support the argument with evidence, not assertion.

Practical example
“We should increase the price of the premium package.”

1. Explain

What does this mean exactly?

“Increasing the price of the premium package means charging more for the highest tier, without fundamentally changing its role.”

2. Elaborate

What important context matters?

“The premium tier offers more advanced features, more service, and is aimed at customers with more complex needs.”

3. Show the reasoning

What is the causal chain?

Higher value for a less price-sensitive segment
↓
Smaller drop in demand after a price increase
↓
Higher margin per sale
↓
Better profitability

4. Justify

Why does that make this the right choice?

“Therefore, increasing the price is appropriate because this tier creates stronger value and can likely bear a higher price.”

5. Substantiate

What evidence supports it?

“WTP data, customer interviews, conversion rates, margin analysis, and competitor benchmarks.”
A strong decision argument is not just a conclusion. It is a structured chain: define the claim, develop the context, show the causal logic, defend the choice, and support it with evidence.
