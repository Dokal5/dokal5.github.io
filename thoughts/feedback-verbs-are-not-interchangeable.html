Feedback Verbs Are Not Interchangeable

Each verb points to a different thinking gap in an argument.

Many people receive feedback but do not know what action it actually requires. The problem is often not effort. The problem is translation. Words like explain, justify, or substantiate may sound similar, but they ask for different kinds of improvement.

Causal Chain Interpretation

Reasoning is the causal core. The other verbs either prepare the chain, conclude from it, or test whether it holds.

Preparation

  • Explain: makes the starting claim precise.
  • Elaborate: adds the relevant conditions and variables.

Causal Chain

  • Reasoning: connects the variables through cause and effect.

Decision Conclusion

  • Justify: turns the chain into a decision conclusion.
Substantiate: tests whether the chain is credible with evidence.

Running Example

Example claim: “We should increase the price of the premium package.”

Increasing the price of the premium package means charging more for the highest tier offer, while keeping the basic package structure and target segment largely the same.
The premium package includes the most advanced features, the highest service level, and the strongest outcome for customers with more complex needs. These customers are often less focused on the lowest price and more focused on performance, convenience, and reliability.
If the premium package delivers significantly more value, and if its customers are less price sensitive, then a moderate price increase is less likely to reduce demand sharply. If demand stays relatively stable while the price rises, the company earns more margin per sale and can improve profitability.
Therefore, increasing the price of the premium package can be an appropriate decision when the offer is clearly differentiated and the target segment values the additional benefits enough to tolerate a higher price.
This should be supported by willingness to pay data, upgrade behavior, customer interviews, conversion rates by tier, margin analysis, and competitor benchmarking.

Causal Bridge

Claim

“We should increase the price of the premium package.”

Causal bridge

Higher value → lower price sensitivity → limited volume loss → higher margin → better profitability

Decision

“Therefore, a price increase is appropriate.”

Substantiate = evidence that the bridge can actually hold.

Key Insight

Many weak arguments do not fail because they lack words. They fail because the wrong function is missing. Sometimes the claim is unclear. Sometimes the context is underdeveloped. Sometimes the causal logic is missing. Sometimes the conclusion is not defended. Sometimes evidence is absent.

Evidence cannot replace reasoning. Data alone does not create an argument. A citation is not a substitute for causal logic.

Final Synthesis

“A strong argument is not just a conclusion. It is a structured chain: clarify the claim, develop the context, show the causal logic, defend the choice, and support it with evidence.”
