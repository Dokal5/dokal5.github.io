Feedback Verbs Are Not Interchangeable

Each verb points to a different thinking gap in an argument.

Many people receive feedback but do not know what action it actually requires. The problem is often not effort. The problem is translation. Words like explain, justify, or substantiate may sound similar, but they ask for different kinds of improvement.

Causal Chain Interpretation

Reasoning is the causal core. The other verbs either prepare the chain, conclude from it, or test whether it holds.

Preparation

  • Explain: makes the starting claim precise.
  • Elaborate: adds the relevant conditions and variables.

Causal Chain

  • Reasoning: connects the variables through cause and effect.

Decision Conclusion

  • Justify: turns the chain into a decision conclusion.
Substantiate: tests whether the chain is credible with evidence.

Build the Bridge

Example claim: “We should increase the price of the premium package.”

Function

Clarify exactly what the claim means before debating whether it is wise.

Content that belongs here

Definition of the proposal, scope, and what is not changing.

Good example

“Increase premium price by 8% while keeping features and target segment the same.”

Common confusion

“Customers might still buy it” (that is reasoning, not explanation).

Function

Add context and decision-relevant variables around the claim.

Content that belongs here

Customer segment characteristics, value drivers, constraints, and market conditions.

Good example

“Premium buyers value uptime and service quality more than minimum price.”

Common confusion

“Therefore we should raise price now” (that jumps to justification).

Function

Build the causal bridge from claim to likely outcome.

Content that belongs here

Linked cause-effect steps, assumptions, and directional impact.

Good example

Higher value perception → lower price sensitivity → limited volume loss → higher margin → better profitability.

Common confusion

“Survey says 62% accept a higher price” (that is evidence, not reasoning).

Causal bridge: this is the logic engine connecting claim to decision.

Function

State the decision conclusion that follows from the reasoning.

Content that belongs here

A clear “therefore” statement with conditions and choice.

Good example

“Therefore, a controlled premium price increase is justified for this segment.”

Common confusion

“Premium customers need reliability” (context, not conclusion).

Function

Test whether the causal bridge is likely to hold in reality.

Content that belongs here

Evidence: willingness-to-pay data, conversion rates, interviews, and competitor benchmarks.

Good example

“Pilot market kept 94% conversion after a 7% premium price lift.”

Common confusion

“The logic seems reasonable” (reasoning quality is not evidence quality).

Evidence layer: substantiation stress-tests the bridge, it does not replace it.

Key Insight

Many weak arguments do not fail because they lack words. They fail because the wrong function is missing. Sometimes the claim is unclear. Sometimes the context is underdeveloped. Sometimes the causal logic is missing. Sometimes the conclusion is not defended. Sometimes evidence is absent.

Evidence cannot replace reasoning. Data alone does not create an argument. A citation is not a substitute for causal logic.

Final Synthesis

“A strong argument is not just a conclusion. It is a structured chain: clarify the claim, develop the context, show the causal logic, defend the choice, and support it with evidence.”
