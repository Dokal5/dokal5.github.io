Strategic Framework · Competitive Advantage

Where does real advantage come from?

Not from the product itself. Not from competition. True competitive advantage comes from one place: delivering progress that customers care about — better, more reliably, and more profitably than anyone else.

Core thesis
Can a company help a specific customer, in a specific context, achieve a progress they care about — and make that value felt, trusted, and repeatedly accessible?

Customers don't buy products. They buy progress — a better version of a situation they're already in.

01 · The Core Insight

They're not buying the product.

Every purchase is really a hire. Customers recruit products to complete a job they couldn't — or didn't want to — do alone.

02 · Six Dimensions of Progress

What customers are actually buying

Progress isn't one-dimensional. A customer's "job to be done" spans six distinct value layers — each one a different kind of improvement in their situation.

⚙
Functional Value
Gets the task done better
The most visible dimension. Does this make things faster, more accurate, more reliable, or easier to execute?
  • Faster outcomes
  • Higher success rate
  • Less effort required
  • More precision
◈
Economic Value
Makes resources go further
Not just "cheaper" — the whole resource equation shifts. Less waste, more leverage, better returns per effort invested.
  • Lower total cost
  • Higher asset utilization
  • Better cash flow
  • Increased revenue potential
◉
Risk Value
Reduces exposure to failure
Customers don't just want outcomes — they want to avoid bad ones. Reducing uncertainty is itself a form of progress.
  • Lower decision risk
  • Fewer failure modes
  • More predictability
  • Downside protection
◇
Psychological Value
Puts the mind at rest
The most underestimated dimension. People aren't purely rational. Feeling confident, calm, and in control is real value.
  • Reduced anxiety
  • Sense of control
  • Feeling understood
  • "I made the right call"
◯
Identity & Social Value
Signals who you are
Purchases are statements. People buy belonging, status, and self-expression — not just utility. Some brands don't solve problems. They confirm identities.
  • Status & prestige
  • Group belonging
  • Values alignment
  • Professional image
▣
Systemic Value
Makes the whole machine run smoother
Especially powerful in B2B. The value isn't one feature — it's reduced organizational friction across teams, processes, and scale.
  • Easier to standardize
  • Simpler to train on
  • Scales without breaking
  • Cross-team coordination
03 · The Analysis Framework

Three layers to diagnose any company

Apply this sequence to any business — including your own — and competitive logic becomes much clearer.

I
Start here
What progress is the customer trying to make?
The real job. Not what they say they want — what situation are they trying to get out of, or into? This is your anchor point for everything else.
Reach a result faster Reduce failure risk Feel professionally credible Regain a sense of order Make better decisions with less effort
II
Then classify
Which value dimension does that progress belong to?
Map the job onto the six value types: Functional, Economic, Risk, Psychological, Identity, Systemic. This tells you what kind of proof customers need, and what competitors can steal.
Functional Economic Risk Psychological Identity Systemic
III
Only now ask
What capability lets this company deliver that value better than anyone else?
This is where competitive advantage lives — not in value itself, but in the structural ability to produce it more consistently, at lower cost, and in ways that are hard to copy.
Technology Brand trust Network effects Data moat Process efficiency Distribution reach Organizational speed Customer relationships
04 · The Critical Distinction

Value and advantage are not the same thing

Most people confuse these. Separating them is the most clarifying move in competitive strategy.

Value is about
Customer outcomes
The result the customer experiences. The progress they feel. The job that gets done. Value is a relationship between product and customer context — it's subjective, situational, and always perceived from the outside.
≠
Advantage is about
Competitive position
Your structural ability to deliver that value more consistently, more cheaply, or in ways harder to replicate. Advantage is internal — it lives in capabilities, not in customer perceptions.
05 · The Precision Principle

Value is never universal

The same attribute can be a strength or a weakness depending on who's evaluating it and in what situation. There's no "generally valuable" — only "valuable for this customer, in this context."

"Low price"
For cost-sensitive buyers
→ Strong value signal
For prestige-seeking buyers
→ Destroys perceived quality
"Fast delivery"
For urgent, reactive needs
→ Core differentiator
For careful, deliberate buyers
→ May signal cutting corners
"Premium quality"
For performance-focused segments
→ Worth paying more for
For pragmatic, "good enough" buyers
→ Over-engineering nobody asked for

The goal is not to find a universal value list — it's to find which value, for which customer, in which context, is decisive.

The complete model
Customer Progress = Functional improvement + Economic improvement + Risk reduction + Psychological stability + Identity confirmation + Systemic fluidity
Competitive Advantage = the ability to deliver the above more consistently, at greater scale, more profitably — and in ways your competitors cannot easily replicate.
