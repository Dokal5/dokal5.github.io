Customer Value, Jobs, and Progress

Before segmentation, messaging, or the marketing mix, we need to know what people are actually hiring the offer to do. The offer is the vehicle. The customer is buying progress.

Core logic

Teams often say what the product is before they can say what progress the customer is trying to make. Marketing works better when we reverse that order.
01 · Foundation

The first mistake is confusing the thing sold with the value bought

A product description is not yet a customer insight. We need to separate the offer itself, the job the customer is hiring it for, the value that matters most, and the implication for market meaning.

Four layers to keep separate

Offer The concrete product, service, or system the firm brings to market.
Job What the customer is trying to get done, solve, avoid, or enable in a specific context.
Value The improvement that matters most: time, certainty, status, confidence, coordination, savings, or reduced risk.
Meaning What the brand should stand for if it truly delivers that progress better than alternatives.

Why this matters for the rest of the course

STP becomes sharper when we know which progress different customers value. Positioning improves when it expresses that progress clearly. The 4Ps become more coherent when they all translate the same value logic into market decisions.

  • Segmentation starts to reflect real differences in what matters.
  • Targeting becomes a choice about who values this progress most.
  • Positioning becomes the market meaning of that progress.
  • The marketing mix turns that meaning into product, price, place, and promotion.

03 · Alignment

Good marketing starts by naming the right progress

When we misread the value being bought, every later step starts to wobble.

Aligned reading

The firm sees that busy professionals are not mainly buying software features. They are buying smoother coordination and fewer operational mistakes.

Result: the position, message, and mix all support ease, visibility, and confidence.

Misread reading

The firm thinks the same buyers mainly want “more tools” and piles on features that do not solve the real job.

Result: messaging becomes feature-heavy, the product feels complex, and the position stops matching the progress customers actually care about.
