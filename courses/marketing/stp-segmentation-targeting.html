STP: Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

STP is not just a sequence of labels. It is a chain of strategic decisions: first recognize meaningful groups, then choose whom to serve, then decide what the firm should mean to that audience.

Working logic
Segmentation identifies differences.
Targeting chooses a priority audience.
Positioning becomes the market-facing response to that choice.

Strategic Meaning

Positioning Is the Response to the Chosen Target

Segmentation and targeting are not the end of the framework. Once the firm chooses which customers it wants to prioritize, it still must decide how it should be perceived by them. Positioning is that decision.

From market choice to market meaning

Segmentation tells us that different kinds of customers exist. Targeting tells us which of those customers the firm will prioritize. Positioning then defines the promise, association, and value logic the company wants that audience to hold in mind.

That is why positioning comes after targeting. A position only becomes sharp when it is built for someone specific rather than for the entire market.

STP logic

  • 1. Segmentation
    Divide the broader market into groups with distinct needs, behaviors, and value expectations.
  • 2. Targeting
    Choose the segment that best fits the firm’s capabilities, economics, and strategic intent.
  • 3. Positioning
    Design the distinct meaning and value proposition the chosen segment should associate with the firm.
Targeting answers

Who are we serving?

Which customer group matters most? Which segment is reachable, attractive, and aligned enough to prioritize over the others?

Positioning answers

How should we matter to them?

What benefit should the target audience associate with us? Why should they choose us over alternatives? What distinctive place should we occupy in their perception?

Positioning as a strategic response

Positioning should be derived from the implications of the chosen target segment. If the target values convenience, the position should emphasize speed, simplicity, and reduced effort. If the target values prestige, the position may instead emphasize exclusivity, design, and status.

  • The segment’s needs indicate what kind of value matters most.
  • The firm’s capabilities indicate what promise it can credibly deliver.
  • The competitive context indicates how the promise can be differentiated.

Example

If the target segment is young urban professionals who are digital-first and willing to pay for ease, a weak position would be “good quality for everyone.” A stronger position would be “the premium everyday option that saves time and removes friction from busy city life.”

The move from ST to P is the move from selecting who to serve to deciding what we want to mean to them.
