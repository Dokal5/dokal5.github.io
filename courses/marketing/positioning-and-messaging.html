From Target Segment To Positioning To Messaging

01 · Source Of Value

Before position and message, we need to know what the audience is actually buying

This page is about moving from target segment to position to message. That chain only works if we first understand the progress the audience is actually trying to make. Otherwise, the position becomes arbitrary and the message becomes empty wording.

What people are actually buying

A drill may be bought for a hole, but often what someone really wants is a finished wall, a calmer home, or the feeling that a long-postponed task is finally done. The product is only the vehicle. The purchase is really a hire for progress. That matters here because positioning should express that progress, and messaging should put it into language the audience can immediately recognize.

Job What is the person trying to get done, solve, avoid, or become?
Value What kind of improvement matters most here: functional, economic, psychological, social, systemic, or risk reduction?

Why the marketing mix matters here

The logic is simple: positioning should guide the marketing mix, and message is one part of that mix.

In other words, message does not decide the whole marketing mix by itself. Instead, the chosen position should shape product, price, place, and promotion, and the message should express that same position in words.

  • Product: makes the promised value real in use
  • Price: signals what that value is worth
  • Place: makes that value accessible in the right context
  • Promotion: explains the value in language the audience can recognize
02 · Core Logic

The relationship to keep in view

Positioning and messaging make sense only after the firm chooses who it wants to prioritize. This works best as a chain of decisions, not as a collection of disconnected labels.

Step 1

Segmentation

Recognize that the market is made of different groups with different priorities, contexts, and expectations.

→
Step 2

Targeting

Choose which segment matters most and deserves the strongest strategic attention.

→
Step 3

Positioning

Decide what the offering should mean to that chosen segment and which difference should matter.

→
Step 4

Messaging

Translate the position into language, emphasis, proof, and tone that the target can actually receive.

04 · Alignment

Good fit versus bad fit

Positioning and messaging are most visible when they go wrong. The same target can receive a message that feels perfectly matched or obviously mismatched.

Aligned

Busy professionals hear a message built for their actual day

The segment, the position, and the message are all pointing at the same pressure: people with overloaded schedules who want less friction and more reliability.

Target Segment Busy professionals who care about speed, reliability, and not wasting attention.
Positioning Choice This option removes friction and helps me stay effective when time is tight.
Message "Built for people who do not have time for friction. Fast onboarding, reliable performance, and support when something matters."
Audience Reaction "Yes, that is exactly my problem. I do not want more features. I want fewer delays."
Misaligned

The same professionals hear a message meant for someone else

The target is still busy professionals, but the message now emphasizes a very different value logic: low price and stripped-down simplicity for budget-conscious buyers. The message is clear, but it is clear for the wrong audience.

Target Segment Busy professionals who care about speed, reliability, and not wasting attention.
Positioning Choice This option should reduce friction and help me perform under pressure.
Message "Everything you need, nothing you overpay for. A practical choice for buyers who want reliable value."
Audience Reaction "Why are you talking to me about cheap and basic? I need something dependable that saves time."
05 · Worksheet

A simple framework

The point is to move from audience choice to communication logic in a disciplined order.

Step 1

Target

Which segment are we prioritizing right now?

  • Who exactly is this for?
  • What matters most to them?
Step 2

Tension

What pressure, problem, or decision matters most to that segment?

  • What are they trying to solve?
  • What risk are they trying to avoid?
Step 3

Position

What should the offering mean to them?

  • What angle of difference matters?
  • What do we want to stand for?
Step 4

Message

How do we express that position clearly and credibly?

  • What headline says it best?
  • What supporting message sharpens it?
Step 5

Proof

Why should the target believe us?

  • What evidence supports the claim?
  • What tone makes it believable?
