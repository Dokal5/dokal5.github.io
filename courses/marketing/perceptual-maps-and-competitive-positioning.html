Positioning should be read in stages: first where a brand sits in the market, then how it separates itself from the brands that appear closest to it.
01 · Foundation

A single perceptual map is useful, but often not sufficient

Traditional perceptual maps usually begin with two broad dimensions. In many categories, price and quality are the right starting point because they reveal the broad competitive structure quickly. But brands that look close together on that first map often need further comparison. That is why positioning should be taught as a layered evaluation process, not as a one-time static picture.

What the first map does well

Locate Show where brands sit in the broader market on price and quality.
Cluster Reveal which competitors seem to occupy similar broad positions.
Compare Establish the overall structure of the category before going deeper.

Why a second map is needed

When several brands sit near each other on price and quality, that first map no longer explains why customers might still choose differently. A second-level map can reveal differences in style meaning, audience identity, and benefit sought.

Step 2 · Zoomed Differentiation

Secondary map for the clustered brands

These three brands remain too close on price and quality to explain the choice clearly. The second comparison therefore shifts to the attributes that better separate them: trend level versus classic orientation, and self-expression versus everyday practicality.

Brands that looked close together on the first map now separate because they stand for different style meanings, address different audiences, and deliver different benefits.
Levi's

Classic expressive balance

Positioning summary

Mid-market denim with strong heritage recognition, positioned between everyday wearability and moderate self-expression rather than at either extreme.

Core customer

Broad adult denim buyers who want a familiar, trusted brand and expect one pair to work across casual, routine, and light style-led settings.

Primary benefit sought

Recognizable authenticity, dependable fit expectations, and low-risk purchase confidence.

Style signal

Classic American denim codes with enough cultural relevance to feel expressive, but not strongly trend-driven.

Functional expectation

Reliable construction, familiar cuts, and repeat-purchase usability rather than experimental washes or fashion-forward silhouettes.

Relative distinction

Compared with Tommy Jeans, Levi’s is less logo-led and less youth-coded. Compared with Replay, it is less fashion-shaped and less premium-styled in everyday wear.

Evidence cues for placement

High brand familiarity, core denim heritage, mainstream distribution, and product lines built around classic fits justify its more classic and moderately expressive position.

Tommy Jeans

Youthful identity expression

Positioning summary

Mid-market denim with stronger youth orientation and higher symbolic visibility, positioned toward self-expression and trend familiarity more than pure denim heritage.

Core customer

Younger casual-fashion buyers who want denim to connect with a recognizable branded lifestyle and signal contemporary taste without entering niche designer territory.

Primary benefit sought

Visible brand meaning, social recognizability, and easy access to current youth-coded style.

Style signal

Cleaner, more logo-aware, and more youth-facing than Levi’s, with stronger emphasis on branded identity as part of the look.

Functional expectation

Expected to perform adequately as everyday denim, but functional reliability is not the main basis of choice; brand image carries more weight.

Relative distinction

Compared with Levi’s, Tommy Jeans is more trend-facing and more overtly identity-signaling. Compared with Replay, it is less product-led and less style-refined, relying more on brand-coded lifestyle cues.

Evidence cues for placement

Younger campaign language, more prominent branded styling, and a stronger connection to casual lifestyle branding justify its higher self-expression and higher trend position.

Replay

Stylized everyday premium

Positioning summary

Mid-price to upper-mid-price denim with a more style-shaped everyday proposition, positioned more expressively than Levi’s but less symbolically than Tommy Jeans.

Core customer

Style-aware everyday wearers who want denim to look sharper and more elevated, but still expect regular use rather than occasional fashion purchase.

Primary benefit sought

Improved silhouette, more polished casual appearance, and a feeling of upgraded everyday denim.

Style signal

More fitted, styled, and design-conscious than Levi’s, but less overtly logo-driven and less youth-coded than Tommy Jeans.

Functional expectation

Consumers still expect repeat wear, comfort, and wardrobe versatility, even though styling and finish matter more than in a purely basic denim purchase.

Relative distinction

Compared with Levi’s, Replay leans more into styled everyday premium cues and more visible design shaping. Compared with Tommy Jeans, it relies less on symbolic branding and more on cut, finish, and the garment itself.

Evidence cues for placement

More fashion-shaped product presentation, sharper silhouettes, and premium-casual styling cues justify its position as a stylized everyday brand rather than a heritage-basic or logo-led brand.

A single perceptual map is useful for identifying the broad competitive space, but it is often not enough to explain true differentiation. When competitors occupy similar price and quality positions, a second-level map using different attributes can reveal how they target different audiences and deliver different benefits.
03 · Alignment

Positioning should be analysed in layers, not as one fixed picture

The first map helps us understand the broad market environment. The second map explains why customers may still separate brands that initially appear similar.

Weak reading

If we stop at price and quality, the three middle denim brands can look almost interchangeable. That flattens real differences in identity, style meaning, and benefit sought.

Stronger reading

Broad market position comes first, but true competitive interpretation often requires a second comparison layer. That is where audience identity and everyday versus expressive value become visible.
