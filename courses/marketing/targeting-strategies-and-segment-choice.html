Targeting Strategies and Segment Choice

Segmentation reveals differences. Targeting decides how the firm will compete across those differences. Teams often confuse the segment itself with the strategy for serving it.

The question is not only which segment looks attractive. The question is also how broadly or narrowly the firm should spread its effort across the market.
01 · Foundation

A target segment is not the same thing as a targeting strategy

After segmentation, the firm still has to decide whether it will serve the whole market similarly, several segments differently, one segment deeply, or even a very narrow personalized slice.

Questions targeting should answer

Attractiveness Is the segment large enough, profitable enough, and worth serving?
Fit Does the firm have the capability, credibility, and economics to win there?
Focus Should resources be spread across many segments or concentrated in one?

Four classic strategies

  • Undifferentiated: broad offer for the whole market.
  • Differentiated: different offers or positions for multiple segments.
  • Concentrated: one priority segment with deep focus.
  • Micromarketing: very narrow or individualized adaptation.
02 · Strategy Selector

Choose a targeting strategy and inspect the tradeoff

Imagine a productivity software firm deciding how to grow. Each strategy changes focus, complexity, and risk in a different way.

Strategy profile

Undifferentiated marketing

One broad offer is pushed to a wide market with limited adaptation.

CoverageVery broad
ComplexityLow
RiskCommoditization
Decision implication

Best when needs are similar and scale matters most

This works when customers are similar enough that heavy adaptation adds more cost than advantage.

  • Useful when the market is relatively homogeneous.
  • Often emphasizes scale, availability, and consistency.
  • Can struggle to create a distinctive position for very different segments.
03 · Alignment

The best target is the one the firm can serve credibly and profitably

Teams often over-focus on market size and under-focus on fit. Strong targeting combines outside attractiveness with inside capability.

Attractive but poor fit

A premium segment may look profitable, but if the firm lacks service quality, brand legitimacy, or channel access, targeting it can create expensive disappointment.

Smaller but stronger fit

A narrower segment can be a smarter choice when the firm's value, economics, and capabilities line up tightly with that group's needs.
