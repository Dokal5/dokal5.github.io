Effective Value Capturing · Pricing Framework

From value
to leverage

EVAC 1.0 teaches you to find value and derive a price. EVAC 2.0 forces you to face economic reality first — and reverse-engineer what you can actually build, sell, and win at.

The core shift
EVAC 1.0 → Value → Product → Pricing → Price
EVAC 2.0 → Target Economics → Price Corridor → Power → Negotiation

Price is not always determined by value — sometimes it is determined by power, and value is just the justification you provide.

03 · What's New in 2.0

Three upgrades that change everything

EVAC 2.0 isn't a longer checklist. It's a different philosophy — analysis under pressure, not analysis in a vacuum.

Constraint
Economics triangle up front
Target Economics at Step 0 locks three variables simultaneously: Price × Volume × Margin. Every subsequent step operates inside this triangle. You cannot escape the constraint — and that's the point.
Intersection
Price Corridor + Volume Check
WTP, cost floor, and competitive benchmarks overlap to define a corridor. Then you must verify: at this price band, is the addressable volume sufficient? If not, the business case is killed before going further.
Execution
Negotiation Space
EVAC 2.0 extends past analysis into execution. Step 6 adds anchor points, concession strategies, and walk-away boundaries — teaching students to negotiate, not just calculate.
04 · The Pricing Power Chain

Structure determines 80% before you sit down

Most people think price is decided at the negotiation table. EVAC 2.0 teaches otherwise: by the time you sit down, the structural conditions have already determined most of the outcome.

I
Start here
Structure
Before any negotiation, hard conditions already frame your position. How many competitors? How substitutable is your product? Who controls the channel? Structure determines whether leverage is even possible.

Structure gives you position; leverage is the actual chips in your hand. Does the customer have alternatives? How high are switching costs? What information do you hold that they don't?

05 · The Critical Distinction

Forward reasoning vs. reverse pressure

The same framework — a completely different cognitive demand. One lets you reason comfortably forward. The other forces you to start with constraints and work backwards.

EVAC 1.0 · Forward logic
Value → Price
Exploratory · 2nd-year
You identify the value, translate it into a product, choose a pricing mechanism, and derive a price. The process is linear and constructive — each step builds on the last. Market reality is checked at the end.
→
EVAC 2.0 · Reverse pressure
Price → Design
Constraint-driven · 4th-year
You start with the economics the market will tolerate, then ask: what can I actually build and sell within that constraint? Infeasible ideas are killed immediately. The design emerges from reality — not from wishful thinking.
06 · Three Key Mechanisms

The mechanisms that make 2.0 different

Without these three mechanisms, Power Layer is decoration. With them, it's a fundamentally different kind of analytical pressure.

Mechanism 01
Power Check
At every step, students must answer three questions before proceeding: What is my structure? Where is my leverage? Where does my negotiation power come from?
Cannot answer → sent back. No proceeding without structural justification.
Mechanism 02
Kill Condition
If there is no clear leverage, switching cost is zero, the customer has many alternatives, or break-even volume exceeds the addressable market — the case is terminated.
Students learn: some businesses simply should not exist. That is a real lesson.
Mechanism 03
Reverse Pressure
From the very start: How much do you want to charge? How many do you need to sell? Why would the market accept that price? Value, Product, and Volume are all reverse-constrained by price.
Turns pricing from an output into an input — the most important mindset shift in 2.0.
07 · Teaching Impact

What students actually learn

The frameworks teach different things — not because the content differs, but because the pressure differs.

EVAC 1.0
Students can "have it all"
The linear process lets students walk through each step carefully and build a coherent story. There is no pressure to eliminate options, no kill condition, no forced trade-off. Value propositions can be idealistic — and often are.
→ Understand the logic, but may not learn to decide
→
EVAC 2.0
Students are forced to choose
Feasibility kill conditions eliminate plans that cannot survive economic reality. Forced trade-offs mean students must give things up — not just add features. Simulated pressure brings the classroom far closer to actual commercial decisions.
→ Learn to make quality decisions under real constraints
The complete model
EVAC 2.0 = Target Economics + Price Corridor + Power Layer + Kill Conditions + Negotiation Space
Pricing power is not what you claim your product is worth. It is the structural ability to defend a price — more consistently, in more contexts, and in ways your competitors cannot easily replicate.
