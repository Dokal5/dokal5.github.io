Value Definition and Capture Boundary

Before a team designs an offer or debates price, it needs to know whether the case is commercially worth keeping alive at all. EVAC 2.0 begins by making the boundary explicit.

Core move: define the value clearly, then eliminate cases that cannot support a workable price-volume-margin logic.

Key question: what customer value exists here, and under what conditions could that value be captured without violating economic reality?

Many commercial teams jump too quickly from value language to offer design. They assume that if customers like the idea, the case deserves investment. EVAC takes a harder line. A commercial case survives only if the value story can support a plausible capture logic once willingness to pay, cost structure, volume requirements, and operating constraints are taken seriously.

That is why this stage combines two jobs. The first is still definitional: what gain, solved problem, avoided cost, or reduced risk matters to the customer? The second is filtering: which opportunities should be stopped now because the economics will not hold, even if the concept sounds attractive in conversation?

What This Stage Does

Job 1

Name the value

Specify the outcome the customer is buying, not just the feature set being offered.

Job 2

Test the case

Ask whether a realistic combination of price, volume, and margin could ever make the case worth pursuing.

Job 3

Set the boundary

Define the conditions under which the opportunity remains commercially alive and the conditions under which it should be dropped.

Value Definition Is Necessary But Not Sufficient

EVAC 1.0 was strong at teaching managers to articulate value. That strength still matters. If the customer gain is vague, every later pricing discussion becomes unstable. But EVAC 2.0 adds a more disciplined question: even if the value is real, can the case survive commercially?

Value Logic

Start by clarifying the gain

What changes for the customer? What progress, savings, confidence, speed, or risk reduction actually matters?

Capture Logic

Then pressure-test the gain

Could that value realistically support a defensible price, sufficient volume, and an acceptable margin after constraints are included?

A value story without a capture boundary creates false hope. The team keeps polishing an idea that should have been filtered out much earlier.

How To Define A Capture Boundary

A capture boundary is not a single number. It is the zone within which the case still works. Below that zone, the opportunity becomes strategically or financially weak. Above that zone, the offer may become unrealistic for the market. The boundary is what tells the team whether it is still reasoning about a real commercial case or just an attractive concept.

  1. Step 1
    Identify the customer value driver. Determine whether the primary gain is revenue improvement, cost reduction, risk reduction, speed, confidence, convenience, or some combination.
  2. Step 2
    Estimate what that gain could support. Ask what level of price or commercial commitment the value might justify in principle.
  3. Step 3
    Add the case constraints. Include cost-to-serve, delivery complexity, sales friction, adoption requirements, and expected volume.
  4. Step 4
    Mark the elimination line. Decide when the opportunity stops being worth pursuing because margin, scale, or execution demands break the logic.

Questions A Team Should Be Able To Answer

Value

What exactly becomes better for the customer, and how important is that improvement compared with alternatives or inaction?

Economics

What combination of price, volume, and cost would make this case commercially credible rather than merely interesting?

Exit rule

What evidence would tell us to stop investing in this case before we waste more design, sales, or management attention?

Typical Failure Modes

  • Treating customer enthusiasm as proof that the economics will work.
  • Using value language that is emotionally persuasive but too vague to support pricing logic.
  • Ignoring the volume requirements needed to make the case operationally worthwhile.
  • Continuing to refine an offer after the economics already indicate the case is too weak.

Why This Comes Before Offer Design

Offer design should not rescue a broken case. If the basic commercial boundary is weak, better packaging will only delay the decision. EVAC therefore places this stage early. It forces teams to decide whether the opportunity deserves architecture work at all.

Once the case survives this pressure test, the next question becomes more constructive: how should the offer, charging logic, and pricing architecture be designed so the opportunity fits inside the workable corridor rather than drifting outside it?

In practical terms, this stage protects managerial attention. It helps teams spend time building offers only for cases that still have a plausible right to exist.
